Refraction & Lenses

Class 10
Lens type

What's going on?

A convex lens is fatter in the middle, so it bends light inwards. Rays arriving parallel to the axis all pass through the focus F₂ on the far side.

Far away → small inverted image (that's a camera, and your own eye). Bring the candle inside F₁ and the rays leave too spread-out to meet — they only appear to come from a big upright image on the same side. That's a magnifying glass 🔍.

A concave lens is thinner in the middle, so it spreads light outwards. Parallel rays leave as if they came from the focus F₁ on the candle's side — they never really meet.

So the image is always virtual, upright and small, sitting between O and F₁. A door peephole works this way, and spectacles for people who can't see far (myopia) use concave lenses too.

Formula

1/v − 1/u = 1/f · m = v/u · P = 1/f(m)

—

Sign rules (New Cartesian): measure from the optical centre O, with light travelling left → right. Left of the lens is negative — so u = −30 cm. Convex f is +, concave f is −.
