Curved Mirrors

Class 10
Mirror type

What's going on?

A concave mirror curves inwards, like the inside of a spoon. Rays that arrive parallel to the axis all bounce through one special point — the focus F. The centre of curvature C sits twice as far, at 2f.

Where the image forms depends entirely on where the candle stands. Far away → a small, upside-down image. Inside F → a big, upright image "behind" the mirror. That's why dentists and shaving mirrors are concave (used up close), and torches put the bulb at F to throw a straight beam.

A convex mirror bulges outwards, so it spreads rays apart. The spread-out rays only appear to meet behind the mirror — the image is always virtual, upright and small, tucked between P and F.

Because it shrinks everything, it squeezes a huge view into a small mirror. That's a vehicle side mirror — with the famous warning "objects in mirror are closer than they appear".

Formula

1/v + 1/u = 1/f  ·  m = −v/u
—
—

Sign rules (New Cartesian): measure from the pole P. In front of the mirror is negative, behind is positive — so a candle 30 cm in front has u = −30 cm.
